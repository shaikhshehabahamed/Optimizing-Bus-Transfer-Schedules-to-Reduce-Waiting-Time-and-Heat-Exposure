# Optimizing-Bus-Transfer-Schedules-to-Reduce-Waiting-Time-and-Heat-Exposure

Extreme heat can make transit transfers unsafe and stressful, especially when riders must wait outdoors with little shade. This study tests whether small schedule changes can reduce both waiting time and heat exposure during bus transfers. Using weekday service data from CARTA in Chattanooga, Tennessee, we combined transfer schedules, stop shade conditions, and a heat stress measure based on temperature, humidity, sun, and wind to estimate the burden riders face while waiting. We then tested modest timetable adjustments that stayed within normal service limits. The results show that better transfer timing can improve rider conditions without added operating cost. The recommended schedule reduced total waiting time by 3.74 percent and heat exposure by 7.44 percent, while keeping all trip shifts within five minutes. The study also found that a small number of unsheltered downtown stops account for a large share of the system’s heat burden, making them strong candidates for future shade investment. These findings show that heat should be treated as an operational issue in transit planning. Agencies can use low-cost schedule changes right away and pair them with stop upgrades to improve rider safety over time.
